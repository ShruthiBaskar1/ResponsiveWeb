September 15 - Ken Jackson
English, Chair: First Folio! The Book That Gave Us Shakespeare

Abstract: First Folio! The Book That Gave Us Shakespeare?is a traveling exhibition on tour from the Folger Shakespeare Library in Washington, DC, designed to teach visitors about the 1623 First Folio of Shakespeare. Assembled seven years after Shakespeare's death, the First Folio includes 36 of his plays?18 of which had never before been published. Without it,?Julius Caesar, Macbeth, As You Like It, The Tempest, and more could have been lost. The exhibition, which also includes panels and digital content, exclusively features First Folios from the Folger Shakespeare Library in Washington, DC, whose collection of 82 First Folios is by far the largest in the world. The nation-wide exhibition was funded in large part by a generous grant from the National Endowment for the Humanities.

Wayne State University, in collaboration with the DIA (which will house the actual Folio), and Detroit Public Libraries, was selected through a grant review process to be the host of Michigan's First Folio.

A number of events are planned as part of the exhibition that runs from March 10 to April 1, 2016.?Wayne State University will be hosting an opening night gala at the DIA on March 10, 2016, a gala that coincides?with an academic conference at the Bernath Auditorium David Adamany Library. The conference brings together Shakespeare scholars from around the state (UM, MSU, Hope, Western, EMU, etc.) Legendary Shakespearean David Bevington from the U. of Chicago will deliver a keynote address as will Professor Mary Crane from Boston University.

There will be an additional WSU Libraries exhibit, including First Folio/Folger materials, outside Bernath Library. The following week, WSU's Professor Emeritus Arthur F. Marotti will deliver a talk on Shakespeare and the Folio for the annu al?Mary Adelaide Hester?talk at Detroit Public Library. DPL will be hosting its own exhibition of Shakespearean materials, including a 2nd?edition Folio. The DIA, in collaboration with WSU faculty, are organizing K12 workshops. And the Hilberry Theater will be staging?Love's Labour's Lost?(January 29 thru March 12). The Detroit Film Theater is planning its programming that month to include a number of Shakespearean adaptations.

This talk will discuss the significance of the First Folio and plans ? still ongoing ? for a celebration of Shakespeare in Detroit for 2016.

Abstract: For most of the past sixty plus years I've lived and studied and written about cities facing the consequences of demographic, economic and social decline: in particular Glasgow, Scotland and Detroit, Michigan. September 22 - Geoff Steven Nathan
English, Professor: Parallelisms Between Language and Music


Abstract: In this talk I will argue that linguistic ‘prosody’ (stress and intonation/pitch) is not a linguistically-specific human ability, but rather simply an instance of what psychologists call ‘entrainment’. Our ability to line up stretches of speech with stretches of melody, based on the alignment of ‘beats,’ which are abstract timing units that correspond with both mental and physical rhythmic behavior is common to musical production (singing, clapping and production of instrumental music) and speech. What is traditionally referred to as stress is simply alignment of the appropriate syllables (defined either by weight in weight-sensitive stress systems, or by location in weight-insensitive systems) with accented beats.

Goswami argues that attention to syllable structure and attention to rhythmic beat in music are the same cognitive task. In addition, she notes that appropriately shaped onsets of rhythmic beats facilitate the ability to entrain (that is, to get the listener in sync with the music being heard). This fact coincides with my claim that the universal preference for CV syllables is driven by the perceptual preference for clearly demarcated beats with a sharply audible onset. In addition Goswami has found evidence for a correlation between dyslexia and difficulty in perceiving speech prosody and syllable stress.

September 29 - Chera Kee
English, Assistant Professor: Diversity in Comics: Xombi and the Curious Case of David Kim

Abstract: Race is at the forefront of the comic book Xombi. Not only is its main character David Kim one of only a small handful of Korean-American main characters in American comics, but Xombi was originally a Milestone title. Launched under the DC umbrella in 1993, Milestone was an attempt by African-American artists and writers to correct the severe underrepresentation of minorities in American comics. After introducing a slate of African-American heroes, in 1994, Milestone added Xombi to their repertoire. Despite critical acclaim, by 1995, Milestone was forced to cancel the original run of the series. Jeffrey A. Brown argues that one of the reasons Milestone comics like Xombi failed was because of their associations with African-American creators and subject-matter: readers assumed Xombi was a “black” comic, when in fact, it was anything but. Exploring the ways in which race is both wholly defining AND seemingly inconsequential in Xombi, this presentation examines some of the conundrums comics producers of the 1990s faced in trying to broaden the repertoire of superheroes available to the American public.

October 21 - Shirin Montazer, Marisa Young ***CANCELLED***
Assistant Professor, Sociology; Assistant Professor, Sociology, McMaster University: Level of Economic Development of the Country of Origin and Work-to-Family Conflict After Migration to Canada


Abstract: Using a sample of employed adults living in Toronto, Canada, this article examines patterns and antecedents of work-family conflict (WFC) among immigrants, relative to the native born. Specifically we test whether the economic development of immigrants' country of origin -- or intermediary country beyond origin, and length of residence?in Canada interact to affect WFC differentially among immigrants. We situate our argument within human capital and role conflict theory, and hypothesize that country of origin and length of residence?impacts the capital immigrants bring to the labor market, and the return to that capital they receive. Discrepancies between the two likely result in stressful work experiences, and thus greater WFC for immigrants, relative to the native born.?Results of hierarchical linear models indicate greater WFC among immigrants from less developed countries compared to the native born. Further analyses suggest that a) higher WFC among this group is most pertinent to immigrants in the country for 10 years or less and those in the country between 11 and 20 years; b) the higher WFC among these groups is partially mediated by the experience of higher work stress; and c) holding all antecedents and controls constant, WFC is significantly higher among men than women from less developed countries who have been in Canada between 11 and 20 years. Immigrants from developed countries of origin do not report significantly different levels of WFC than the native born.

October 28 - Kristin O'Donovan
Political Science, Assistant Professor; Learning After Disaster

Abstract: The purpose of this project is to determine whether policy failures are a mechanism for policy learning and ultimately diffusion. It is well established in the diffusion literature that states, cities and counties learn about and adopt policies that have been considered successes by their neighbors. However, the role of perceived policy failures has not been investigated as extensively as successes. The policy learning literature indicates that governments can and do learn after a perceived policy failure. This paper asks two questions. First, is policy failure a mechanism for diffusion? Second, if policy failure is a mechanism for diffusion, is the type of policy learning that occurs an important factor? Using the policy learning literature as an organizing framework, this work analyzes two instances of perceived policy failures: the school collapse during the Moore Tornado of 2013 and the residential development damage in the Oso Mudslide of 2014. Using local government data, this research tests for learning of policies designed to prevent future failures to neighboring municipalities. Findings suggest that repeated policy failures are associated with social policy learning and ultimately diffusion.

November 3 - John Corvino

Professor and Chair, Philosophy: Bake Me a Cake: Emerging Conflicts Around Religious Liberty, Tolerance, and Discrimination

Abstract: Now that same-sex couples may marry in all 50 states, the “culture wars” have largely shifted to conscientious objectors: clerks who don’t want to issue marriage licenses to same-sex couples, photographers who don’t want to take their photographs, bakers who don’t want to bake their cakes, and so on. The controversies often arise not because of marriage law, but because of anti-discrimination law, which in some jurisdictions covers sexual-orientation discrimination.

Consider for example Sweet Cakes by Melissa, which was charged with violating the Oregon Equality Act and ordered to pay $135,000 in damages after declining to provide a wedding cake for a lesbian couple. Many observers are conflicted about this case: On the one hand, they appreciate Oregon’s efforts to combat discrimination in public accommodations. On the other hand, they worry about trampling the bakers’ liberty to choose their commissions?and even more so the liberty of photographers in related cases, where free-speech concerns are more conspicuous.

In this talk, Prof. Corvino will critically examine five alternatives for balancing our commitment to equality and nondiscrimination with our commitment to personal liberty as we approach the “cake wars.”

Abstract: Ireland is an unusual country that freely considers foreign applicants for senior government positions.  Stephen Calkins successfully applied for appointment as a Member of Ireland's Competition Authority, which was scheduled to (and eventually did) merge with Ireland's National Consumer Agency to form the new Competition and Consumer Protection Commission.  Professor Calkins has agreed to share reflections on his experience.  It was a challenging time for Ireland, which had committed to a disastrous bank bailout that backfired badly.  Ireland was something of a ward the European Troika (the European Commission, the European Central Bank, and the IMF).  At the same time, a country that was 84% Catholic was working through fallout from abuse scandals, and relations with the UK and with Northern Ireland were a work in progress.  Today Ireland has emerged from the bailout process but is still deeply in debt; Northern Ireland is posing new challenges; and the Catholic Church was largely marginalized during the extraordinary referendum that legalized gay marriage.  Within the Irish Government Calkins experienced the very different functioning of an Irish enforcement agency and legal and political system.  Time permitting, Calkins will also offer observations about politics, the press, and life in Dublin.

November 10 - Katheryn Maguire

Communication, Associate Professor: Communication, Paradox, and the Long Distance Experience 

Abstract: Long distance relationships are a common occurrence. Whether in dating relationships, military marriages, or transnational families, relational partners must find ways to cope with the challenges of maintaining close emotional ties across a distance. In particular, relational partners may encounter times when the message they send to accomplish one goal (e.g., cope with stress, express affection) results in an unexpected or negative outcome. These paradoxical outcomes, in turn, may further impede the family member’s ability to stay connected with loved ones. In this presentation, the concept of the “communication paradox” will be discussed within the context of long distance family relationships. By understanding that these paradoxes are often context and time dependent, family members may recognize the paradox is not indicative of a problem with the relationship; instead, it is an outcome of the situation, and thus may not reoccur when the family is out of the stressful situation.

November 11 - Stella M. Resko

Assistant Professor, School of Social Work & Merrill Palmer Skillman Institute: Changes in Marijuana Policy, Perceived Risk and Adolescent Marijuana Use

Abstract: Marijuana is the most commonly used illicit substance and U.S. marijuana policy and public opinion are rapidly evolving. Currently, 23 states have enacted laws making medical marijuana legal and 4 states have enacted state laws legalizing recreational marijuana use for adults. This presentation examines the growing political debate on marijuana policy and whether the legalization of medical and recreational marijuana results in increased access and use among adolescents. We review recent trends in adolescent marijuana use and empirical evidence on the impact of marijuana use among adolescents. We then describe the results of our recent statewide survey (N=560) on the perceived risk of adolescent marijuana use in Michigan. Our findings suggest adults generally had fewer concerns with adolescent marijuana use that was not associated with other problems (e.g. failing grades, school suspension, and marijuana possession arrest). Adults were more concerned by vignettes that involved solitary marijuana use or use in multiple contexts (e.g. social settings and alone). We conclude with a discussion of how shifts in marijuana policy and the perceived risk associated with marijuana impact social workers and other substance use prevention and treatment practitioners.

November 18 - Robert Sedler

Distinguished Professor, Law School: The Constitution and Marriage Equality

Abstract: This is Professor Sedler’s third presentation at the Humanities Center Brown Bag Colloquium Series on the subject of the Constitution and marriage equality. December 8 - Sarah Swider
Assistant Professor, Sociology; Race, space and Gender: African American Women Entrepreneurs in Detroit's Informal Economy

Abstract: This study looks at the haircare industry in one Detroit neighborhood as a way to explore how economic opportunities for black women in Detroit have changed over time and how it has impacted neighborhoods. I have chosen the haircare industry because of its historical importance. In part, demand for beauty services among African American women has been fueled by society's insistence that African American women conform to white mainstream beauty ideals. This demand has been satiated by entrepreneurial African American women who have dominated the industry with successful and thriving beauty salons. These salons have played an important role in creating economic stability for African American families, economic advancement for African American women and vitality in African American communities. Using a mix of qualitative and quantitative data, this study reveals significant changes in the industry in one neighborhood, showing how registered storefront salons have disappeared and been replaced by "kitchen salons" in private homes. It explores why this change has happened and what the effects have been on these women, their families and the community.

December 9 - Marc W. Kruman
Professor of History and Director of the Center for the Study of Citizenship: Citizenship in Detroit in a Time of Bankruptcy

Abstract: Once the driving force of American manufacturing and a vast city of two million residents in 1950, Detroit's economy crumbled and now barely 670,000 residents dot 140 square miles of land. Fifty years of economic and demographic decline--combined with opaque, outdated and sometimes corrupt City operations--found the City financially insolvent and unable to deliver basic city services. Many streetlights went dark; an inadequate police force patrolled inadequately; and buildings burned in the face of a short-staffed fire department.

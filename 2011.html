Theme: The Post-Industrial City

The Advisory Board of the Humanities Center invites WSU faculty to propose papers that address the ideas inherent in the concept of The Post-Industrial City, explicated below.

Conference Date: November 18, 2011.

Explication

Since the 1960s, large industrial cities in Northern Europe, the (former) Soviet Union, and the United States have undergone profound economic, social and demographic changes. Manufacturing, once the lifeblood of these cities, has declined dramatically; suburbanization and shrinking inner-city populations have led to the urban decay we now associate with the "post-industrial" city.? While the economic effects of industrial decline have been well documented, far less scholarly attention has been paid to the effects of post-industrialism on the arts and humanities.

During the first half of the 20th century, industrial cities generated a disproportionate share of the growth of national economies.? In a climate of economic innovation, success, and profitability, the arts flourished, as is clear from the proliferation of (small) theater companies and art museums, the expansion of art collections, and the development of new and experimental forms of music and art. The working class cosmopolitanism of Liverpool produced the Beatles, and Detroit's industrial economy of the 1960s helped to establish the Motown music industry. This period taught us that strong local economies enable and encourage new developments in the arts. New developments in art and art practices, reflecting local cultures and tastes, circulate more broadly in the global economy and bring recognition and resources back to the city in a cycle of creativity and growth.

To be sure, post-industrial cities continue to produce and enjoy artistic innovations. Techno-music is thriving in Detroit, while new-wave and hip-hop are flourishing in Manchester. But post-industrial cities are not the centers of artistic development. London still dominates British culture, but London itself, while clearly cosmopolitan, is decidedly non-industrial. Liverpool, a post-industrial city, is considered a backwater of British culture.

With the gradual disappearance of influential arts and artists from the former industrial centers, the position of the “culture industry” has changed as well. Many post-industrial cities no longer see the arts as valuable in and of themselves?as an organic part of socio-cultural developments and trends more generally. Instead, art is largely seen?and actively employed/exploited?as a tool for economic development, public relations, and merchandizing. The mid-town district of Detroit, for example, emphatically foregrounds the arts to attract visitors, to seduce new residents, and to promote its struggling economy.?

There are other imaginative ways to look at, and even employ, the arts and the humanities. Urban planners argue that the key to reviving post-industrial cities is in redefining what it means to be "urban." Once associated with overpopulation and over-use, "urban" now means engaging in "smart growth," preserving historic areas for repopulation, and promoting Green enterprises that create new and sustainable lifestyles. Essential to this regeneration is the development of an inner-city core where various races, ethnicities, classes, and age groups live, work, and build new cultures together.? The new urban city is an "incubator" for creative activities and innovations, including advances in communications and technologies essential for growth in a global economy. The humanities, arts and social sciences can and should play a central role in this regeneration.?

Keynoter/Abstract

Art in Post-Industrial Detroit
Marion Jackson, Distinguished Professor
Emerita, Art and Art History, Wayne State University

(Video)

Arts and cultural institutions in Detroit have suffered the "perfect storm" of drastically diminished government and corporate support, a "tanked" economy, widespread urban blight, and a steadily deteriorating population base. In the midst of apparent collapse, however, Detroiters seem to know instinctively that the arts ? and the resourcefulness, sense of shared humanity, and energy that the arts engender ? are essential to the healing of our community and to renewing its social and economic vitality. Flagship cultural institutions have responded to the economic downturn by expanding their focus on the community.

The Detroit Institute of Arts has worked to broaden its constituency, offering free Fridays for Detroit residents and a range of cultural events that attract a diverse public. The Detroit Symphony Orchestra ended a season-long strike by offering free concerts in public venues around the community. Midtown Detroit, Inc. and the Detroit Riverfron Conservancy have catalyzed arts activities along the Woodward spine and the waterfront. And young artists ? many working "under the radar" and outside traditional cultural institutions ? bring great life and energy to the City and attract others with resonant hearts and minds to join them ? reclaiming Detroit's reputation as a place of creativity, energy and new possibility. I will focus on some of Detroit's creative artists who operate essentially "under the radar" and will discuss Wayne State's new volunteerism-in-the-arts initiative, ArtsCorpsDetroit ? offering a reflection on the changing role of the arts in our City and in our times.

Speakers/Abstracts

Making Music, Again: An Assessment of Detroit's Contemporary Neighborhood Music Industry
Robin Boyle, Professor, Urban Studies & Planning, Wayne State University
Matthew Lewis, Graduate Student, USP.

(Video)

The history of popular music in Detroit has been well documented (Smith, 1999; Reynolds, 1999; Kramer, 2002; Gibson 2003; and Quispel, 2005). Particularly well known is the saga of Motown Records and the impact of the relocation of the record label from Detroit to Los Angeles. More recently, new attention has been paid to Detroit's music scene. There has been considerable interest, not least from Europe, in the development of contemporary music?hip-hop, garage, and techno?and its inter-racial performers and audiences. Despite the considerable popular literature on this phenomenon there is little discussion about an embryonic musical industry in the city and its connection with and to the local economy.

Set into the context of citywide economic dislocation and physical decline, this paper and presentation will trace the evolution of a small neighborhood-based music industry in Detroit. Based on survey data and a series of interviews with key musicians, producers and entrepreneurs, the "new Motown" will be described and evaluated. Various dimensions of the phenomenon will be analyzed, including the segmentation of the cultural economy in the city. The paper will conclude with an assessment of the contribution this music economy may make to the restructuring of the city.

The Poetics of Destruction
Melba Joyce Boyd, Distinguished University Professor, And Chair of the Department of Africana Studies

(Video)

"The Poetics of Destruction" will be a presentation that will chronicle the physical and social demise of Detroit as expressed through poetry. In addition to my own work, I will read and contextualize poems by other Detroit poets in order to illuminate the nuances of the transformation of the city in ways that are generally ignored by traditional scholarship. In particular, the emotional and the subjective onslaught of human experience relative to historical and social economic conflict will be the thematic focus of the presentation.

Imaging Detroit: Between Surface and Depth
ren?e c. hoogland, Associate Professor, English

(Video)

This paper will look at the ways in which Detroit, not so much as a city needing new cultural impetuses, but as in and of itself a site for the visual construction of the post-industrial city functions in such a way that it raises questions about the categories of city, culture, and representational art as such. In this paper, I will explore recent visualizations of D-troit in order to question such images as representations of something else, and suggest that we focus, instead, on the function of such images as pictorial surfaces that do "their own work," over and above the fact that they (also) refer to some underlying or ulterior, pre-existing, or referential reality. D-troit, by far the most visually represented image--or nightmare--of the post-industrial city, will be presented as a key "moment" in the revolutionary changes that are happening in the realm of the visual at this increasingly global, digital, and post-postmodernist moment.

Music in the Post-Industrial City: Detroit's Cass Corridor--A Jazz Retrospective.
Frank F. Koscielski, Academic Services Officer IV, Labor@Wayne and Marick Masters
Scott Gwinnell, Jazz Composer, Graduate of Wayne State University's Music Department

(Video)

In light of the present economy, many artist-entrepreneurs are turning to emerging funding sources and products to provide money to do the work they want and have to do. The ultimate post-industrial city, Detroit, has more than its share of detractors and yet the music and musicians of the city persist and thrive with remarkable talent and creativity. This presentation will tell the story of one such artist, jazz pianist and composer Scott Gwinnell, and his unique project: "a jazz interpretation of the buildings, structures, people, cultures and artifices" of the Cass Corridor. The project will result in a CD produced by Detroit's own Mack Avenue Studios, entitled Examining Detroit's History Through Jazz. Mack Avenue Records has agreed to pay the mechanical costs of the CD along with promoting the work. Money to pay for the recording and production of the CD, including paying the musicians, has been raised through the use of "Kickstarter," which bills itself as the "largest funding platform for creative projects in the world." Gwinnell is not alone in seeking crucial funding in this manner, and in his quest to gain supporters, he has also widened his audience and the potential cultural impact of the project.

Our Humanities Center presentation will tell the story of this project from inception to fund raising to completion with Frank Koscielski of Labor Studies and the artist, Scott Gwinnell making the presentation as a team.

Pittsburgh's Cultural Strategy
Tracy Neumann, Assistant Professor, History

This paper investigates efforts by Pittsburgh's political and business elites to create a cohesive cultural district adjacent to the city's central business district and promote (and subsidize) new museums and leisure activities throughout the city in the 1970s and 1980s. I argue that the city's elite-led growth coalition worked to dismantle the popular image of Pittsburgh as a working-class, industrial city by remaking urban social life and cultural space and by rebranding Pittsburgh as a "headquarters city" rather than a steeltown. The growth coalition saw the creation or expansion of high-culture amenities such as art museums, theatres, the symphony, and the ballet as part of an economic development strategy that would draw new white-collar residents to the city and attract the disposable income of tourists and suburbanites. By pairing a narrow focus on white-collar, FIRE-sector job creation with an explicit cultural strategy, members of the growth coalition worked to redevelop Pittsburgh for an imagined new white-collar citizen and pushed existing residents to the margins of civic life. The paper will address what kind of "culture" (and for whom) a cultural strategy seeks to produce, and what arts-based economic development strategies meant for working-class urban residents as manufacturing jobs disappeared in the 1970s and 1980s.

"Zero" - A novel of repression and brutalization in Brazilian Cities
Renata Wasserman, Professor Emerita, English and Comparative Literature

(Video)

Large industrial cities were a phenomenon not only of Europe and the United States, but also of other parts of the world, in so-called underdeveloped areas, where they contrasted even more dramatically with rural and small-town areas and cultures; just as they grew with world industrialization, so did they suffer the problems of industrial decay, often compounded by the rise of dictatorships (Buenos Aires, S?o Paulo). These cities were centers of artistic innovation; the economic and industrial problems imposed great pressure on the arts, and at the same time created grounds for the rise of a literature of dystopia, set in decaying urban landscapes, subject to the violence of politics and of the economy, as well as of pressures, economic, political, and cultural from the 'developed' part of the world. As an example, I will talk about "Zero" (a novel of repression and brutalization, whose central character works as a rat-catcher for movie houses, set during the Brazilian dictatorship) by Ignacio de Loyola Brand?o, a contemporary Brazilian writer, four of whose novels have recently been translated into English and published by the Dalkey Archive Press.

New Mobility and the Post-Industrial City
Susan Zielinski, Managing Director, University of Michigan Transportation and Research Center and Sustainable Mobility & Accessibility Research & Transformation (SMART).

The ways in which we move around and between cities and regions is transforming fundamentally. As the world urbanizes, globalizes, ages, and faces major economic, social, and ecological challenges, new forms and systems are emerging for moving (and for moving less) more elegantly, sustainably and connectedly. How does this relate to discourse on the post-industrial city? On the one hand, information technology and new service and business models are beginning to enable more seamless, multi-modal, IT-enhanced, affordable, spatially integrated, and sustainable transportation experiences door-to-door. On the other hand, evolved manufacturing is called upon to supply evolved components of the New Mobility. This presentation will explore visions and examples of the emerging New Mobility as they relate to discussion on the post-industrial city.

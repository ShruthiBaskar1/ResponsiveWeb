Richard Grusin, Chair, English
“Affect, Mediality, and Abu Ghraib”
There is general agreement that the photographs depicting torture and humiliation of Iraqi prisoners at Abu Ghraib reveal actions by US soldiers that are completely beyond the pale of acceptable civilized behavior. Most accounts of these photographs focus on the symbolic or ideological nature of the photographs as representations of the injustice, if not the obscenity, of the US prosecution of the war in Iraq and US power generally. While not disagreeing with these accounts, I want to take a look at another element of the photographs that has been minimized or ignored--the way in which they produce an affective, bodily response that occurs prior to, and is structurally independent of, their cognitive or ideological import. The intensity of this response is heightened, I argue, not because the acts depicted in the photographs are so alien from our everyday experience, but because (among other reasons) the "mediality" of the photographs--the way in which the images were captured and shared across global, digital media networks--is of a piece with our own everyday experiences of digital photography, emailing, and browsing the web.

Denver Brunsman, Assistant Professor, History
“The American Revolution's First Victory: Ending British Naval Impressment in North America, 1763-1769”
Historians rarely include impressment, or forced service into the British Royal Navy, among the great issues of the American Revolution such as sovereignty, taxation, representation, and independence. Yet between the Glorious Revolution of 1688 and the Stamp Act Crisis of 1765 no other issue caused as much protest and violent disturbance by American colonists against British imperial officials. Moreover, after the American Revolution the impressment of American sailors would return to be a leading cause of the War of 1812 between Britain and the United States. Why, then, didn’t naval impressment play a bigger role in causing the Revolution itself? This paper explores this question by analyzing British naval logbooks, newspaper reports of riots in American seaports, and the diary and legal papers of John Adams. These and other sources point to a single conclusion: by 1769 American seamen and their maritime communities had largely defeated their old nemesis, impressment. Professor Brunsman will explain the importance of this victory and why it is often overlooked by historians of the American Revolution.

Arifa Javed, Lecturer, Sociology
“Paces and Patterns of Acculturation Among post-1990 Immigrants in America”
This presentation is an attempt to understand the changing tone of integration from a one-way process of assimilation to a two-way process of acculturation and reciprocal acculturation among post-1990 immigrants to America. It predicts a change in the texture of the American social fabric in light of this influx of immigrants who are different than their predecessors in terms of race religion and ethnicity. Its findings indicate that though each of the six diverse ethnic groups studied has its own pace and pattern of integration, each meets the requirements of American pluralism.

Avis Vidal, Professor, Geography and Urban Planning
“Interactions between Race and Space: South Africa and Detroit”
The United States and South Africa share long histories of black oppression by whites. The legacy of those histories include enduring inequalities of income and education between blacks and whites and high levels of residential segregation on the basis of color in the major cities in both nations. Centers of employment are increasingly decentralized in both places. Population density is low in South African cities as it is in Detroit and other cities built out in the age of the automobile.

Despite these similarities, American and South African cities are mirror images of one another. Detroit exemplifies all too well the typical U.S. pattern in which white flight left the urban core with large concentrations of black residents while the suburban fringe is primarily white. In contrast, blacks in South Africa were consistently kept out of the central city and black urban residential areas were pushed to the periphery. The seminar will use a comparison of Johannesburg and Detroit to explore the development of this contrast and its implications.

Catherine Barrette, Associate Professor, Romance Languages and Literatures
“Scholarship Within Language Program Direction”
Language program directors (LPDs) may play many roles in their departments: researchers, teachers, teacher supervisors and trainers, curriculum and test developers, program administrators, human resources directors, mediators, advisors, technology support staff, webmasters, and so on. Despite the range of these roles, most LPDs are trained to conduct their research solely or primarily within a branch of foreign languages such as linguistics, literary studies, or cultural studies. While the questions asked within these disciplines and the research methods used to answer them can be quite varied, they do not necessarily address issues that are particular to LPDs’ non-instructional roles. This paper explores some of the research questions and methods used in other disciplines such as industrial and organizational psychology, educational administration, and business management that can enhance LPDs’ approaches to research. Input from attendees regarding the applicability of these and other disciplines’ research models would be most welcome.

Lisa Maruca, Associate Professor, Interdisciplinary Studies
“Text/Trade/Technology: Constructing Print Literacy in Eighteenth-Century England”
This presentation will examine the changing relationship between pedagogical reading and writing wrought by new educational technologies in late eighteenth-century England. Student writing in earlier period had been seen as a physical skill based in the act and art of copying others’ text, beginning with individual letters and moving to complete essays. This “copy-writing” worked through a set of specific somatic techniques and technologies to literally internalize cultural norms and ideals within the student body. In this context, reading was a process meant to set off other processes, such as the oral production of knowledge through recitation or the hand’s role in the collection and reinscribing of text within student commonplace books.

Significant changes in the methods of literacy training, however, emerged in the eighteenth century England not only from education theorists but from a print trade eager to create a demand for educational books. Because of the growing acceptance of a specifically print literacy that demanded no interaction, the student reader began to be imagined as the subordinate member of an hierarchal relationship, poised to absorb passively the superior mind of the literary or scholarly author. The technology of the book thus suppressed the student hand and voice and disaggregated reading and writing.

Fred Wacker, Associate Professor, Interdisciplinary Studies
“Reflections on Gambling in Detroit: Humanistic Costs and Benefits”
The vast bulk of studies of gambling seek to measure two phenomena? The economic “costs” and “benefits” and the incidence of “problem” or “pathological” gambling. In this talk, I wish to sketch out some parameters of the humanistic impacts of gambling behavior and the gambling life. Few observers doubt that gambling has been a persistent part of “American” culture since the Founding, including the popular lotteries that provided venture capital in the early Republic. Horse racing, which today will be propped up in most states by so- called “racinos,” is another deep vein of popular culture. What many find most surprising is the recent and nearly total loss of influence of gambling prohibitionists. Although prohibitionists still may wield the power of stigma against drugs and some victimless crimes, gambling spreads like wildfire. Only two states, Utah and Hawaii, today have neither legalized gambling nor lotteries. Native American gaming is spreading Chance and Lady Fortune into remote areas of the nation.

Worse, from the standpoint of those concerned with new addictions and the fate of souls, may be the spread of internet gambling. Those linked to the Web may gamble at work or home and I doubt that the courts will be able to police this form of risk-taking. But, some may ask, how do we as humanists distinguish internet or casino risk taking and speculation from day trading and commodities brokerage? Does gambling really accelerate and intensify appetites or contain and express them?

Some humanists have argued that gambling excursions, the buses packed with the silver haired and lame, are hardly to be feared. The elderly, ought to be allowed their penny and nickel slot machines. Their casino outings allow for unharmful and cheap thrills. On the other hand, many college administrators and professors, having jettisoned in loco parentis in the dust some decades ago, now appear most puzzled by the fervid appeal of “Hold-Em” poker. Jackson Lears, in his book Something for Nothing, makes rather large claims for the necessity of appeals to Chance and Fortune to counterbalance the increasing power of rational thought to enclose modern life in its “iron cage”.

Anne Rothe, Assistant Professor, German and Slavic Studies
“Constructing Post-Holocaust German Identities in Israel/Palestine, 1946-2004 -- An Oral History Project”
This paper presents an overview of an oral history project which examines an unexplored aspect of post-Holocaust German-Jewish relations: the motivation for and experiences of non-Jewish or converted Germans living temporarily or permanently in Israel/Palestine.

The individual motivations for and durations of living in Israel are diverse, ranging from marriage and religion to study and volunteering; and from a few months to several decades respectively. However, all of my interviewees (had to) reflect on their collective identity of being German and not Jewish or converted in a Jewish State or the Palestinian Areas and thus under Israeli occupation, on German collective memory and on German/Jewish relations in a post-Holocaust time.
This project is grounded in theories of individual and collective memory and self-identity and contextualized in historical research on post-Holocaust German-Jewish and German-Israeli relations. It employs oral methodology, which will be integrated with the newly emerging genre of life writing.

Together with my interviewees I explored questions of nationality, ethnicity and religion in our shared effort to understand what it means to be a German in Israel today, and, more generally, what it means to be German in a post-Holocaust age.
Since I was not interviewing an Other but rather was in a similar subject position as my interviewees, a German Gentile in Israel, my research is integrated with critical self-reflection on my own motivations for doing the project.

The interview transcription cost and a preliminary analysis of select interviews was generously funded in the Summer of 2005 by a Humanities Center Grant.

Barrett Watten, Professor, English,
“Franco Luambo Makiadi's Universalism”
In this paper, I take up the invocation of a cultural universal--that everyone should get together to "Stop AIDS!"--in Franco Luambo Makiadi's Afro-pop tour de force Attention na Sida, perhaps the most brilliant public service announcement ever made, a warning against AIDS that has been described as “the most intense 16 minutes 37 seconds ever recorded, and the most courageous." Franco, who died in 1989, most likely of causes related to the disease, was widely known as le grand ma?tre of rumba-soukous, a cultural figure who achieved a scale of recognition comparable to that of the major actors in the postcolonial history of Congo/Za?re, from Kasavubu and Lumumba to Mobutu. Franco achieved this recognition by means of a popular art that began with a synthesis of local knowledge and imported idioms, defined a distinctive genre at a parallel moment to national liberation, was redefined in conditions of exile and emigration, and ended with a hybrid. global aesthetic articulated at the scale of a social discourse. Due to historical conditions of reception, Franco’s work is much less known to Americans than it should be, particularly in comparison to the Nigerian oppositional icon Fela, even while there is a consensus among historians of Afro-pop of the enormous scale of Franco’s influence. As a result of its form of cultural innovation, Attention na Sida both intends and realizes a poetics of universal address, written from a position of postcolonial displacement, that allows us to reassess the status of universals through the specifics of history, genre, and local knowledge.

Amy Adamczyk, Assistant Professor, Sociology
“Friends' Religiosity and the Timing of First Sex”
A number of studies have found that more religious teens have sex later than less religious adolescents.? Researchers have also found an inverse relationship between friends’ attitudes and teens’ sexual behaviors. However, little attention has been given to understanding the relationship between friends’ religiosity and first sex.? Specifically, are adolescents influenced by their friends’ religiosity ?that is, a socialization process? Or do they sort into friendship groups on the basis of consistency between their sex behaviors and the group’s religious attitudes ?that is, a selection process? Drawing on ideas from symbolic interactionism, I explain why we would expect these relationships.? Using information from teens’ friends and data from the National Longitudinal Study of Adolescent Health (Add Health), I test the influence of friends’ religiosity on first sex, and the effect of virginity status on moving to a more or less religious friendship group.? Net of individual religiosity the association between friends’ religiosity and first sex is reciprocal; effects operate in both directions.? I discuss how these findings relate more generally to what we know about friendship group processes.

Steven Palmer, Associate Professor, History, University of Windsor
“Slavery and the Production of Medicine in Cuba, 1800-1880”
Cuba had a greater?density and distribution of titled medical practitioners than anywhere else in Latin America during the nineteenth century, and indeed Havana’s community of titled medical practitioners was among the densest in the world.? One of the main reasons was the high demand for medical attention for slaves at a time when the supply of new slave bodies was falling and their price rising. ?The talk will explore how this multi-layered community of practitioners, and their access to a large clinical mass of slave bodies, produced a complex world of academic medicine centered in Havana.? The universe created by plantation medicine was central to the development of proto-national medical institutions and leaders.

Mary A. Wischusen, Associate Professor, Music
“Franz Schubert and the Popular Style”
The most popular theatrical experience in Vienna during the 18th and early 19th centuries was the folk comedy, whose origins date back to at least the Middle Ages. Featuring magical plots, farcical situations, local color, and improvised dialogue, this genre appealed to all classes of society.? Although the popular comedy was often performed in humble settings and was at times censored and even expelled from theaters, it continued to develop, eventually merging with Viennese singspiel and later with German romantic opera.

Franz Schubert lived his entire life in and around Vienna, where he was a choirboy and student, and later a freelance composer who longed for success with stage music.? He is known to have attended performances of two farces at the Leopoldst?dtertheater, the most important venue for popular comedy at the time.? It is likely, however, that Schubert attended more performances and absorbed the conventions and music of the popular theater into his personal style.? Some of Schubert’s early stage works reflect the unpretentious musical styles of the popular stage, although these are sometimes juxtaposed against more sophisticated idioms.? Later stage works, too, reveal influences from the popular theater.? Even his mature instrumental works and lieder betray touches of the popular style.

This paper will provide a short history of Viennese popular comedy and chronicle the role of music.? It will then focus on the influences of the characters, situations, and musical styles of this genre on Schubert’s music, particularly on the stage works.

Anca Vlasopolos, Professor, English
“American Patriotism: Nathaniel Bowditch and the End of Monarchy at Sea”
Nathaniel Bowditch came to maturity during the American Revolution. Born and raised in Salem, MA, Bowditch was a self-educated mathematical genius who sought to reform navigation by translating the complicated calculations of chronometers, sextants, compasses, and maps into reference tables that any sailor with a minimal training in math would be able to read. His accomplishments were many, but his name still lives among ordinary sailors as well as in the annals of international navigation because of his tome?Bowditch’s New American Navigator, dubbed the Bible of navigators. Later in life, Bowditch spent most of his personal fortune on translating and publishing LaPlace’s M?chanique c?leste, then the most advanced book on astronomy, “for the benefit of my countrymen.” My project is to bring to life through experimental narration the life and times of this remarkable man, who shaped concepts of American patriotism in a crucial period of nation building.

John Reed, Distinguished Professor, English
“Personification in Dickens”
Personification may be viewed in various ways.? In this paper I mean by personification the animating of inanimate things, particularly giving them human-like traits.? Dickens uses this technique frequently in his writing.? My argument is that he uses it consciously as a non-realistic mode of perception with the purpose of retaining strict command of his narrative.? He emphasizes his own use of personification, by employing the opposite device of de-animating human beings.? Although Dickens can write in a realistic manner, this fanciful technique and other devices keep him intentionally out of the realist camp.

Frances Ranney, Associate Professor, English
“Technology as Surrogate: Mothers, Daughters and ‘Labor-Saving’ Devices”
As Ruth Schwartz Cowan points out in More Work for Mother, technologies created to do household tasks do not always reduce labor in the home.? In fact, they often increase it by simultaneously increasing standards of cleanliness, a result that Cowan maintains is (or was) purposeful.? So-called ‘labor-saving’ devices, she says, encouraged women’s return to the home at the end of World War II so that soldiers could take up what had been “men’s” jobs before the war created a labor crisis.? Other, specifically feminist, work in technology studies maintains that women interact with technologies differently than men do (see Machina ex Dea) or that technologies affect women’s lives differently than they do men’s (Technology and Women’s Voices).

Work on women’s relationships with technology as a “surrogate” tends to discuss reproductive technologies (see, for example, Women and Reproductive Technologies).? This talk will consider technology literally as a surrogate for human relationships, focusing primarily on the mother-daughter relationship in the home as it is conducted through household technologies prior to the digital age.? Drawing on work that claims technology increases the distance between women?and other work that claims it brings women closer?I will suggest that when household technologies brought women’s work into the isolation of the home, they fostered the development of a new, perhaps closer but not necessarily harmonious, relationship between mothers and daughters.

Sandy Pensoneau-Conway, Basic Course Director, Communication
“Eros in the Classroom: Desire and Passion as Foundations of Teaching and Learning”
What do we think (or feel) when we hear the word “erotic”?? Madonna?? Exotic dancing?? Cinemax late-night movies?? What do we think (or feel) when we hear this word in the context of education?? To speak of anything “erotic” in the classroom seems to bring nearly immediate discourse of immorality.? In this lecture, I trouble that conceptualization of Eros, and instead broaden the concept to include its historical roots in something more contemporarily akin to “love” and “emotion.”? I reclaim the concepts of Eros and the erotic by reflecting on two notions of it: one within the confines of an expanded notion of sexuality, and one broader conceptualization based on the historical story of the god Eros and the Freudian notion of Eros.? I then argue for a shift in pedagogical practice from resisting Eros as something immorally sexual, to embracing Eros as an affirming, creative, and relationship-building pedagogical necessity.? I conclude by offering the basic communication course classroom as a prime context for embracing Eros and Eroticism.

Robert Sedler, Distinguished Professor, Law
“The Constitutional Protection of Religious Freedom and the Religiosity of the American People”
Religious freedom is a favored value in the American constitutional system. It is the first guarantee of the First Amendment, which begins with “No law respecting the establishment of religion or prohibiting the free exercise thereof.”? The First Amendment provides a two-fold protection to freedom of religion by what are referred to as the “religion clauses.” The Establishment Clause protects against the “establishment” of an official church by the government and against governmental action “establishing religion.” It requires that the government maintain a course of complete official neutrality toward religion. The government cannot favor one religion over another religion or favor religion over non-religion, such as by requiring prayer or bible reading in the public schools, or by using public funds for religious purposes, The Free Exercise clause is a textual guarantee of peoples’ right to practice their religion and to hold and act on religious beliefs, free of governmental interference. It invalidates laws that expressly discriminate against religion or against people because of their religious beliefs, such as a law that disqualified members of the clergy from serving as legislators, or a law that required a declaration of a belief in the existence of God as a test for holding public office.

The Establishment Clause has been the more important of the two clauses. The underlying theory of the Establishment Clause is that prohibiting the government from trying to advance religion serves to protect the religious freedom of all of us, and particularly of religious minorities. This point is often not fully understood by religious believers, who may see such restrictions as demonstrating hostility to religion. It is not a matter of hostility toward religion, but rather a structural concern for religious freedom that prohibits the government from using its power to advance religion. The Supreme Court has interpreted the Establishment Clause broadly and has invalidated laws that have either the purpose or effect of advancing religion. The Free Exercise Clause has been less important in protecting religious freedom, because the Supreme Court? has not interpreted it expansively. The Court has held that as a general proposition,? the Free Exercise Clause does not require that the government exempt religiously motivated conduct from neutral and generally applicable criminal laws, so that, for example, it is constitutional for the government to prohibit the use of peyote in the religious services of the Native American Church.

However, because religious freedom is a favored value in the American constitutional system, the Constitution permits the government to undertake affirmative efforts that are precisely tailored to protect the religious freedom of individuals and religious institutions. It does not matter that the failure of the government to take such action would not violate the Free Exercise Clause. So long as the action is directed toward obviating an interference with religious freedom that the government itself has imposed, it may be constitutionally permissible and does not amount to a preference for religion. Thus, Congress has exempted the use of peyote in the religious services of the Native-American Church from federal substance abuse laws and has allowed Kosher and Halal animal slaughter that would otherwise be prohibited by the federal Humane Slaughter law. Title VII’s prohibition against religious discrimination in employment requires an employer to make a “reasonable accommodation” for an employee’s religious beliefs, so long as this can be done without undue hardship on the conduct of the employer’s business. States must, as a condition of receiving federal funds, make a “reasonable accommodation” for the religious practices of prison inmates. There are man other examples of constitutionally permissible action designed to protect the religious freedom of individuals and religious institutions.

Many years ago, Supreme Court Justice William Douglas stated that, “We are a religious people whose institutions suppose the existence of a Supreme Being.” By every measure, such as church membership, contributions to religious organizations, religious-based positions on public issues Americans appear to be more religious than their counterparts in other western democracies such as Canada, Great Britain, France, Italy, Germany, and the Netherlands. It is also fair to say that while these countries provide some degree of constitutional or legal protection to religious freedom, this protection does not approach the extensive constitutional protection to religious freedom provided in the United States.

Professor Sedler maintains that constitutional doctrine can influence values held by the society at large. Recognizing that there are many explanations for the greater “religiosity” of the American people compared with the people in other western democracies, Professor Sedler proposes that the colloquium explore the relationship between the greater constitutional protection of religious freedom in the United States and the greater “religiosity” of the American people.

“(Re)vision of a Life: My Mother's Holocaust Story”
The Holocaust was a crime of such proportions and consequences that a focus on individual lives and deaths best serves to bring us closer to comprehending its scale and scope. Such a focus also ensures the preservation of both individual recollections and collective memory for future generations and serves to counteract Holocaust denial. Authentic oral and written survivor accounts continue to emerge, and it is vital that they do so because the opportunities for testimony will soon be gone. She shared her experiences with me in ever more specific detail, cognizant of the need to leave them for posterity and collective memory.

My memoir of my mother’s Holocaust experiences and exile will contribute to the literary tradition, “from mother to daughter,” that has found a special place in Holocaust testimony literature, or literary memoir--or the “hybrid of history with memoir” called “storia.” Everything in my personal and professional life has led to this memoir project. This presentation, part of a work--and a journey--in progress, is dedicated to my parents and to my husband, Marvin S. Schindler, all of blessed memory. Kathleen McCrone, Professor Emerita of History, University of Windsor
“Victorian Women and Music”
This paper examines the increasingly active and varied roles played by middle-class women in the musical revolution that swept through England during the second half of the nineteenth century, and relates them to the parallel movement for the emancipation of women.

Although the feminist movement never took up the 'woman in music' question as a specific cause, its challenges to perceptions of women's abilities and "proper sphere" contributed to women violinists, oboists, and composers arriving side by side with women physicians, university graduates, and scientists.

An exploration of such topics as domestic music, music education, the instrumental music profession, and musical composition reveals conflicting images and realities. While women played increasingly significant roles in England's amateur and professional musical life by 1914, these roles remained severely restricted. Music's highest realms, indeed virtually the whole musical establishment, remained in the control of men--professors, performers, conductors, composers, producers, agents, impressarios, publishers, and critics. Only certain aspects of musical activity were considered appropriate for women and compatible with socialization for femininity, while much of what women did musically, as in other spheres, continued to be viewed as trivial and unworthy of serious consideration.

Ultimately the debate on women's musical abilities and proper place in the late-Victorian world of music reflected the tensions evoked by the many challenges to traditional roles and stereotypes that were occurring at the time. The debate was certainly far from settled when the Great War began. The classical music profession's continuing patriarchy testifies to this remaining the case.

Ronald Brown, Associate Professor & Wassim Tarraf, GTA, Political Science
“Is Racial Solidarity Possible Among African-Americans and Caribbean Blacks?”
This paper attempts to contribute to the emerging scholarship that focuses on the relative saliency of a “shared racial group identity” between American born blacks and black immigrants from the Caribbean. More pointedly, we study the idea of a “master status” dominating other “status identities” among American blacks. We accomplish this by engaging in two interrelated tasks. First, we construct a schematic, structural equation, model of black group identification and test it on both an African American and an Afro-Caribbean sample from the National American Life Survey, 2003. This allows us to examine whether “racial identity” overwhelms “ethnic identity” or whether different historical and cultural experiences change the saliency of the racial identity among members of the two groups. We argue that the racialized nature of American society leads to a common racial identity model for both groups. Yet, we show that the complexities of the racial and ethnic identities manifest themselves differently in the structural relationships (causal effects) within the model. We contend that the different economic, cultural, political and social experiences of the African and Afro-Caribbean Americans, as measured through our proposed constructs, feed differently into the “common” racial script of the two groups. Second, we take our own argument of a racialized American society to task. We investigate the systemic nature of racialization by testing how black ethnics become racialized as African Americans .We retest our proposed model on a generationally split Afro-Caribbean sample and show evidence of what Clair Kim (2000) calls “minorization of immigrants.”

Marvin Zalman, Professor, Criminal Justice
“Edwin Montefiore Borchard (1884 - 1951): Seeking Justice for the Wrongly Convicted at Yale”
Edwin Borchard's popular book of vignettes, Convicting the Innocent (1932), is widely cited in contemporary law review articles as the earliest significant American work on the wrongful conviction of factually innocent persons. On the surface, it seems odd that a leading national expert in international law would delve into the functioning of police, prosecutors, and criminal courts.

Why did Borchard, a Yale Law School professor, undertake the project that led to Convicting the Innocent? In fact, indemnity for the wrongly convicted was a longstanding interest of his. Other factors also stimulated the project, including the urging of his friend, Felix Frankfurter, Borchard's progressive attitudes, and the influence of the legal realist movement at Yale.

The references to Convicting the Innocent in the law journals rarely allude to the author's efforts in the years following its publication. Borchard used his considerable energy, contacts, and influence to conceive of, and successfully lobby for passage of federal legislation to compensate wrongly convicted persons. The failure to stimulate comparable state legislation, however, was indicative of the lack of public concern about convicting the innocent in the years before DNA exonerations became common.

Jennifer Olmsted, Assistant Professor, Art and Art History
“Admiring the Enemy? French Artists and the North African (Anti) Hero. 1830-1850”
After France invaded Algiers in 1830, French artists rushed to visit the Maghreb for the first time in history. Spurred on by the French public’s thirst for information and by the government’s demand for official paintings, artists produced hundreds of images depicting Algerians. Despite the predominance of negative representations of North Africans, significant exceptions portrayed them in positive terms as brave and honorable. I will focus on the challenges posed by a series of unusual portraits to our current understanding of imagery produced in an era of colonization.
Four individuals received particularly idealizing treatment at the hands of French artists: Ali ben Hamet (khalifa of Constantine), Yussuf (colonel and eventually general of the spahis), Mohammed ben Abou ben Abd el-Malek (commander of the Moroccan Royal Cavalry), and Abd el-Kader (leader of the Algerian resistance). Given the context of war and colonization, how can such admiration be interpreted? Post-colonial theory ? and the studies it has inspired ? argues that the colonizer’s representations of the colonized always reveal a will to domination. A less totalizing approach, I argue, will enhance our understanding of the images of the four men in question here. Linked by admiration, the representations of these men embody different responses from the artists, ranging from the triumphalist to the subversive. By examining the ways in which these men were idealized and the artists’ motivations for doing so, we can better understand the relationship between imperialism and image-making.

Richard Marback, Associate Professor, English
“Unclenching the Fist: Regarding Rhetoric's Embodiment”
In February 2004, in response to the shooting deaths of two Detroit police officers, two men from Redford poured white paint over Monument to Joe Louis. At their arraignment one of the men explained the vandalism by saying they had wanted to “unclench the fist.”
The claim to want to unclench the fist may sound like a desperate attempt to justify an indefensible act. It may well be that. But it is also an expression that raises questions about the distribution of rhetorical agency among people and objects. As iconoclastic gesture, vandalism of The Fist aimed to diminish the representational force of the monument. Instead the vandalism raised questions about whether the monument represents Black Power and where that representation resides. Does it reside in The Fist itself? Or is it a representation that resides in the people who view it? These questions are important for rhetorical studies because they introduce concerns about the meaningfulness of objects and the potential in those objects to enable and constrain our expression. Using vandalism of The Fist as my point of reference I argue for understanding expression as an embodied encounter with objects.

This is a preliminary examination of the classical influences upon the life of William Pickens (1881-1954). No secondary scholarship on this topic is available and no full-scale biography has yet been attempted. Irshad Altheimer, Assistant Professor, Criminal Justice
“Firearms and Crime: A Cross-National Approach”
This study examines the relationship between city levels of gun availability and individual crime victimization. It is argued here that existing theoretical approaches to guns and crime can be integrated with opportunity theories to provide a richer understanding of the dynamic between guns and crime. Data for this analysis are drawn from a sample of 45,913 individuals nested in 39 cities in developing nations. The results of the hierarchical linear models tested here indicate that city levels of gun availability influence individual odds of gun crime victimization but not individual odds of overall crime victimization. These results suggest that gun availability is not necessarily a cause of crime. Rather, levels of gun availability influence the odds that guns will be used during the commission of a crime.

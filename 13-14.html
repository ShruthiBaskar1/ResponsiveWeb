September 04 - Michael H. Scrivener
English, Distinguished Professor. The San Dominque Slave Rebellion and Feminist Reform in John Thelwall's "The Daughter of Adoption"
Abstract: I have co-edited a scholarly edition of John Thelwall's 1801 novel, The Daughter of Adoption, recently published by Broadview Press. In my paper I will concentrate on two aspects of the novel, how the representation of the Saint Domingue slave rebellion functions in relation to the overall feminist theme, embodied in the novel's heroine Seraphina. Aristocratic male sexuality and social privilege are represented as forms of domination, not just of working-class women and enslaved women but also of upper-class women, like the protagonist Henry's mother. The novel contrasts the predatory sexuality of the privileged males with the female-centered educational reforms of the "petticoat philosophers" like Anna Barbauld. The popularized representation of the Saint Domingue rebellion emphasized black male violence against whites, especially white women. Thelwall subverts these racist misrepresentations by writers like Bryan Edwards and Baron de Wimpffen in a number of ways: the slave rebellion itself, despite the sexual violence, is aligned with the French Revolution; the violence of the rebels is depicted as discriminatory rather than merely vengeful; the plantation elite are seen as far crueler than the rebelling slaves. Moreover, the novel's sensationalistic images of slaves raping white women?images made iconic by writers like Edwards?are countered by numerous other images of white domination, exploitation, and injustice. The overall plot hinges on the moral reformation of the aristocratic male hero, Henry, who will not be allowed to marry Seraphina until he adopts fully the cultural values of revolutionary feminism.

September 17 - Ljiljana Progovac
English Department and Linguistics Program, Professor and Director. Detroit's Language Evolution and Linguistic Fossils: How Humans Spoke at the Dawn of Language, and Why our Grammars still Preserve "Fossils" of such Speech
Abstract: It is widely acknowledged that we live in a increasingly visual world and yet knowledge concerning visual information continues to lag behind that of the printed or spoken word among the general population. This new visual paradigm, driven in large part by technological developments enabling the easy exchange and display of images, has brought into sharper focus the lack of knowledge and training concerning visual information. September 18 - Scott C. Richmond
Film and Media Studies English Department, Assistant Professor. Vulgar Boredom: On Time, Detachment, and Some Boring Films by Andy Warhol and Christopher Nolan
Abstract: Philosophers of boredom such as Heidegger and Agamben have tended to theorize a split in boredom, between a vulgar boredom arising from everyday life under capitalism or modernity, and an authentic, profound boredom, a path philosophy. This split is mirrored in the critical discourse on Warhol. Works like his serial silkscreens, or Empire and his other early "stillies" - these are good-boring, commenting on the bad-boring of ordinary commodity objects and deadening mass culture.

In this paper, I consider Warhol's profoundly-boring Kiss (1963) alongside Christopher Nolan's vulgar-boring blockbuster Inception (2010). To be sure, many found Nolan's film captivating; my own boredom is rather data for a phenomenological and psychoanalytical theorization of boredom as an affective and aesthetic disposition for film viewers. What happens when we watch boring media? And why should we want to do so?

Using psychoanalyst Thomas Ogden's scheme of psychic positions, I argue that boredom as aesthetic response belongs to the depressive position, marked by a sense of distance from the world. This depressive distance is itself ambivalent, as it can lead either the possibility of aesthetically redeemed profound boredom, attended by critical or interpretive distance; or, it entails the slackened, alienated, and affectively reparative vulgar boredom.

September 24 - Brady Baybeck and Adam Uddin
Political Science, Associate Professor & MPA Director; Political Science, Doctoral Student. Power to the Powerless? Adopting District Representation In Detroit
Abstract: In November 2009, voters in the City of Detroit, Michigan, passed Proposal D by a nearly 3 to 1 margin. This initiative changed the structure of representation in the city from a nine-member city council elected at-large to a hybrid system with seven districts and two at-large.

Although one could certainly argue that the city had bigger issues to deal with, this new form of representation indicated a break from 100 years of stability in governmental structure. Thus it is worthwhile to examine why this proposal made it on the ballot and why it passed so easily. In this paper, we focus on the latter question, examining the factors that led to the successful election outcome. We find that passage of this proposal was in some sense inevitable ? there were many factors that pointed in the direction of a move to district representation. This finding runs somewhat contrary to the conventional wisdom, which argues that success came from a well-run campaign that reached out to the various constituencies of the city.

This suggests that the process of getting on the ballot is more important than the actual campaign and election ? that, in cities like Detroit, it is the agenda-setting that drives the process.

September 25 - Robert Ackerman
Law School, Professor. Corporate Communitarianism
Abstract: The United States Supreme Court's decision in Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission 558 U.S. 310 (2010) held that a federal statute, 2 U.S.C.A. ? 441(b) (2002), restricting corporate and labor union expenditures to defeat a candidate for office could not withstand first amendment scrutiny. While applauded by an unusual coalition of first amendment absolutists and conservative politicians, the decision has been derided by advocates of election reform and liberal critics, including the President of the United States.

Largely overlooked in this discussion have been the implications of Citizens United for corporate law, and in particular, the extent to which state corporate law permits for-profit business corporations to use corporate funds to engage in political discourse and activities. By declaring that corporations (and unions) have first amendment rights coextensive with those of other citizens and ascribing "personhood" to corporations, the Court majority may have (albeit inadvertently) trumped state corporate laws and behaviors that narrowly restrict the scope of corporate conduct to the maximization of shareholder profits. And the Citizens United decision may have provided encouragement to communitarians who, given our belief that rights imply responsibilities, will urge that there now remains little excuse for corporations to deny accountability to constituencies beyond their shareholders. Unfortunately, it may have also given license to corporate officers who might view the corporate fisc as a means of financing their own political beliefs and aspirations, to the detriment of other corporate constituencies.

Together with Professor Lance Cole of Penn State University's Dickinson School of Law, I am exploring how Citizens United may have not only reordered the law pertaining to corporate speech, but further weakened the "shareholder wealth maximization" theory of corporate activity. We will consider the evolving role of corporations (from the early days of the Ford Motor Company to the "Don't Be Evil" philosophy of Google) along with the potentially benign effect of cases expanding the free speech rights of corporations and unions.

October 02 - Robert Henderson
English Department & Linguistic , Assistant Professor. Language death, language revitalization, and the development agenda
Abstract: Healthcare NGOs working in indigenous language communities are in a unique position to serve as allies in language maintenance and revitalization efforts. On one hand, there is evidence providing care in a patient's first language improves health outcomes (Aranguri and Ramirez 2006). On the other hand, healthcare NGOs work in a high-status domain of language use important for language revitalization (Heinze Balcazar 2012).

In this talk I report on the work of Wuqu' Kawoq, a nongovernmental health organization that was founded in 2007 with the explicit mission of bridging the quality in health care divide for indigenous Kaqchikel Maya communities by developing health care services and programs delivered natively in the Kaqchikel language. October 08 - Jorge Chinea
History, Associate Professor. Slavery and Free Labor at the End of Spanish "Constitutional Rule" in Puerto Rico 1937-1860
Abstract: Napoleon Bonaparte's invasion of the Iberian Peninsula from approximately 1808 to 1814 had major consequences for Spain and its American colonies. The French occupation forces toppled Spanish King Ferdinand VII, triggering a popular outcry characterized by the formation of juntas or governing bodies that sought to rule on behalf of the deposed monarch. Eventually, the regional assemblies created a Supreme Junta and later a Council of Regency that in 1810 took the unprecedented step of summoning delegates from the metropolis and Spanish America to a parliamentary meeting known as the Cortes Generales y Extraordinarias. Besieged by the stalking enemy and under pressure to build a broad coalition that might preserve the disarticulated empire in 1812 the joint assembly adopted a liberal constitution that remained in effect on and off during the first four decades of the 19th century, specifically between 1812-14, 1820-23, and 1836-37. This presentation explores two specific moments?1812 and 1838?when constitutional decision-making and/or the liberal thinking it unleashed-- impacted on the future viability of servile labor regimes in Spanish colonial Puerto Rico.

October 09 - Robert P. Holley
School of Library & Information Science, Professor. Self publishing for fun, profit, and your scholarly influence
Abstract: In this talk, I will share the insights from having edited a special segment on self publishing in Against the Grain. Self publishing has become increasingly respectable for both commercial and scholarly purposes as the new found popularity of ebooks has opened up markets for aspiring authors and scholars. The astounding success of 50 Shades of Gray shows how a self published book can go main stream with enormous financial rewards for its author. Self publishing is also an alternative for serious scholarly works that do not have the sales potential that university presses and trade publishers increasingly demand. October 23 - Zach Brewster
Sociology, Assistant Professor. Black-White Earnings Gap Among Restaurant Servers: Consumer Discrimination in Tipping Behaviors
Abstract: Objectives: In this talk Professor Brewster will elaborate on a line of research that is only in its infancy stages of development and yet is ripe with opportunities to advance our understanding of the effects of consumer discrimination on racial earnings inequities among tip dependent employees in the United States. Methods. Brewster will analyze data derived from a large exit survey of restaurant consumers (n=378) in an attempt to replicate, extend, and further explore the recently documented effect of service providers' race on consumers' tipping decisions. Results. He finds that both White and Black restaurant customers discriminate against Black servers by tipping them less than their White coworkers.

Further, Professor Brewster finds no evidence that this Black tip penalty is the result of interracial differences in service skills possessed by Black and White servers. Conclusion. Racial discrimination in tipping is a salient source of economic racial inequality that has been overlooked by most scholars. As such, Brewster will conclude this talk by delineating directions for future research.

November 06 - ren?e c. hoogland
English, Associate Professor. Playing (on) the Self: Claude Cahun and the
Aesthetics of Un/Becoming
Abstract: French photographer and writer Claude Cahun (1894-1950) cuts an unruly figure in the context of literary criticism and art history. While considered odd, obscene, a genius nonetheless, at the time she created her bestknown works, in more recent years, Cahun has been associated with High Modernism and Surrealism, or, alternatively, claimed for an affirmative (feminist) gender politics.

Rather than argue against such, in my view, reductive approaches, what I will do in this paper is reflect upon the interplay of aesthetics and affect in Cahun's recalcitrant faux autobiography Aveux non avenus
(1930), and in her photographic self-stagings, to expose the mobility, the proliferous, and mercurial nature of a self that is never one, and never once and for all, but, instead, a continuously shifting configuration of fragments and collages without any claim to truth, seriousness, and authenticity. I will further indicate the ways in which her works do not so much represent "anything" but rather effect an alternative mode of relating (to self, to the world, to art) that defies both the parameters of scientific reason, identity politics, art (historical) aesthetics, and political nihilism.

November 13 - Lianqing Peng
Sociology, Visiting Professor. The Effect of Inter-Regional Migration to Regional Economy Disparity in China
Abstract: China's initiation of economic reform in 1978 along with a more open policy towards the global economy, has led to massive internal migration. Most of these migrants are Chinese peasants who have moved from the countryside to large coastal cities. This massive migration has resulted in profound changes in China's economic development and social structures. This paper explores the effects of this massive migration on regional economy disparity.

Neo-classical economics suggests that the process of migration works to narrow economic disparity. In contrast, new economics of migration and dual-labor market theory complicate the picture and suggest that migration, depending on the conditions, could narrow or widen regional economy disparity. This paper uses the interesting case of China, to explore the impacts of migration, specifically inter-regional migration, on economic disparity.  The study uses the Chinese national census and sample survey to analyze labor migration trends and characteristics of these migrants which is then used to analyze changes in regional economies. Results indicate that the inter-regional migration is helpful in improving national economy growth and narrowing regional economy disparity, and it suggests this is due to the more efficient allocation of labor. Furthermore, these findings support further elimination of institutional obstacles that are impeding labor mobility.

January 14 - Stephen Chrisomalis
Anthropology, Assistant Professor. "What's so Improper About Fractions?": Mathematical Prescriptivism at Math Corps
Abstract: Mathematical prescriptivism is a systematic language ideology found in school mathematics that uses a discourse of rationality to approve or disapprove of language forms based on their perceived logicality. The present study is based on a three-year ethnographic investigation of the Math Corps, a mathematical community of practice based in Detroit, in which mathematical prescriptivism is used both to highlight algorithms seen as beneficial and to build social solidarity. Although motivated by the analogy with English spelling reform, prescriptivism at Math Corps must avoid potentially harmful criticism of students or their cognitive capacities, of the sort often experienced by African American students in school settings. A playful linguistic frame, the prescriptive melodrama, is identified and discussed as a means of teaching prescriptions, thereby enculturating students into the locally preferred register, the 'Math Corps way', which encompasses social, moral, and mathematical practices as well as linguistic norms.

Guest Speaker Biographies

Sander L. Gilman will become a distinguished professor of the Liberal Arts and Sciences at Emory University in 2005. For 2004-5 he is the Weidenfeld Visiting Professor of European Comparative Literature at Oxford University. A cultural and literary historian, he is the author or editor of over seventy books. His first biography Jurek Becker: A Life in Five Worlds appeared in 2003 and his widely reviewed monograph Fat Boys: A Slim Book appeared in 2004; his most recent edited volume is Smoke: A Global History of Smoking (with Zhou Xun) also appeared that year. He is the author of the basic study of the visual stereotyping of the mentally ill, Seeing the Insane, published by John Wiley and Sons in 1982 (reprinted: 1996) as well as the standard study of Jewish Self-Hatred, the title of his Johns Hopkins University Press monograph of 1986. For twenty-five years he was a member of the humanities and medical faculties at Cornell University where he held the Goldwin Smith Professorship of Humane Studies. For six years he held the Henry R. Luce Distinguished Service Professorship of the Liberal Arts in Human Biology at the University of Chicago and for four years was a distinguished professor of the Liberal Arts and Medicine and creator of the Humanities Laboratory at the University of Illinois at Chicago. During 1990-1991 he served as the Visiting Historical Scholar at the National Library of Medicine, Bethesda, MD; 1996-1997 as a fellow of the Center for Advanced Study in the Behavioral Sciences, Stanford, CA; 2000-2001 as a Berlin prize fellow at the American Academy in Berlin. He has been a visiting professor at numerous universities in North America, South Africa, The United Kingdom, Germany, and New Zealand. He was president of the Modern Language Association in 1995. He has been awarded a Doctor of Laws (honoris causa) at the University of Toronto in 1997 and elected an honorary professor of the Free University in Berlin.

Karmen MacKendrick MacKendrick is an Associate Professor of Philosophy at Le Moyne College, a Jesuit institution in upstate New York. MacKendrick explores the intersection of the human body and language in her highly acclaimed books Counterpleasures (1999) and Word Made Skin: Figuring Language at the Surface of Flesh (2004). Her work Immemorial Silence (2001) draws on philosophy, theology, and literature, from the early Middle Ages to the present, to explore the function of silence in communication.

Marylin Yalom is a senior scholar at Stanford University's Institute for Research on Women and Gender. Her books have been translated into 20 languages and in 1991 she was decorated as an Officier des Palmes Academiques by the French Government. Her books include Maternity, Mortality, and the Literature of Madness (out of print), Blood Sisters: The French Revolution in Women's Memory (1993), A History of the Breast (1997), A History of the Wife (2001) and Birth of the Chess Queen (2003).

Abstracts

Sander L. Gilman (Oxford University)
“Glamour and Beauty - Imagining Glamour in the Age of Aesthetic Surgery”

Since we now live in an age in which we can refashion our bodies what has happened to the idea of “glamour.” Are our manipulated bodies “glamorous”? Or are they merely mechanically reproduced copies of truly glamorous bodies And who has those bodies? Reality television as well as high art and theory all offer insights into our new claims about the beauty and glamour of our bodies but are they not just more products of the glamour culture?

Karmen MacKendrick (Le Moyne College) "Dismembered Divinity"

Canonizing record-breaking numbers of new saints, Pope John Paul II holds them up as exemplars with whom the faithful and potentially faithful can identify, because the saints resemble the rest of us. This paper examines the role of relics (pieces of bodies or objects that were in contact with those bodies) in the veneration of saints, primarily the newer saints, and argues that the easy sense of identity as a stable core with a fairly stable collection of associated characteristics, which the Pope seems to assume, is actually significantly threatened by the ways that these body bits are made, displayed, and understood. To make this claim, I note three forms of memory involved in the presentation of relics: historical, communal, and sacred. Each of these threatens a simple sense of ego boundaries; the second troubles also the notion of individuality, and the third can undo a sense of "self" at all. The corporeality of relics is bizarre, being highly fragmentary yet evoking a vitality and power beyond that to which we commonly think of bodies as being limited, and kinds of memory that recollect for us unusual modes of wholeness.

Marilyn Yalom (Stanford University) "The Female Body as Cultural Icon: The Earth Mother, the Virgin Mary, and the Chess Queen"

The explosion of interest in prehistoric goddesses in the 1970s and 80s was an attempt to valorize female figures from the past and reclaim them as a source of inspiration. These figures were seen as proof of a time when fat women were revered, and obesity considered a blessing. Similarly, women in the 70s and 80s rediscovered the Virgin Mary, though Mary incarnated a different corporeal reality, more spirit than substance. Only her nude breast in Nursing Madonna paintings and sculpture indicate that she, too, was made of flesh. Despite early Christianity's negative attitude toward the body, Mary's nurturing breast links her to the sacred goddess figures revered 20,000 years before her birth. The connection between the cult of the Virgin Mary and the chess queen revolves around the fact that they entered into history at the same time, around the year 1000, grew up together during the Middle Ages, and reinforced each other. As icons of female power, the mother goddess, the Virgin Mary, and the chess queen are reminders of the various roles women have played throughout the ages--as creators and nurturers of life, as sources of spiritual inspiration, and as formidable political figures.

Thomas Abowd (Anthropology) "Dangerous Bodies/Vulnerable Nations: Arabs and Muslims in a post-'9/11' America"

This paper will examine "national security" policies, civil liberties, and corporeal politics as they relate to Arab and Muslim communities in a post-"9/11" America. I will explore the ways in which bodies have been regulated, ruled, and represented in an age of heightened fear and uncertainty. I have chosen Detroit and its environs as the primary site of my ethnographic work, the home of one of the largest concentrations of Arabs and Muslims outside of the Middle East. This project will be both a critique of institutional forms of power and racism as they relate to these particularly vulnerable communities, as well as an exploration of everyday forms of discrimination, violence, and resistance. I will analyze the post-9/11 legal and legislative landscape in the US with regard to national security and will detail the daily, lived realities of Arabs and Muslims in the city and beyond in the wake of September, 2001. I want to ask how escalating fear, suspicion, and insecurity (nationally and internationally) have impacted these groups in their local, everyday encounters with other Americans.

Robert Aguirre (English) "Embodying American Indigenes: The Case of Julia Pastrana"

This paper examines the case of Julia Pastrana, a hirsute Mexican woman who was exhibited as a human freak on U.S. and European stages during the 1850s, and then again after her death?this time as an embalmed corpse. What meanings were attached to the singular body in such entertainments, and how did these meanings intersect with those drawn from anthropology, which in many cases relied on the same bodies of/for data? How did these conjoined disciplinary twins shape views about the indigenous peoples of the Americas?

Lisabeth Hock (German and Slavic Studies) "Shades of Melancholy in Gabriele Reuter's From a Good Family (1895)"

Gabriele Reuter's 1895 novel, From a Good Family, recounts the story of Agathe Heidling, whose teenage fantasies and unrecognized talents give way in young adulthood to pathological fear and sadness. This presentation will examine these symptoms through the lenses three discourses: artistic notions of the melancholic temperament, late-nineteenth-century psychiatric accounts of clinical melancholia, and Freud's understanding of melancholy as psychic loss. This reading demonstrates that the novel refuses to take sides in the nature vs. nurture debate. Rather, From a Good Family offers a nuanced account on the etiology of mental illness; it considers seriously the premises of late-nineteenth-century biological psychiatry while making a persuasive argument for the connection between societal discourse and women's psychiatric distress.

Ellen Barton (Linguistics) "Experimenting on the Body"

The body is the object of medicine, the subject of its discourse. Healing the body is the ostensible objective and primary relationship between physicians and patients in medicine, but this basis of the profession is built upon many occluded relationships and troublesome discourses. One of these vexed relationships with a problematic discourse is the need for the healing relationship with the body and its patient to be based upon medicine's investigative relationship with the body and the body politic. In medicine, experimenting on the body is an imperative underlying healing the body, and being able to experiment on the body is one of the less acknowledged parts of the social contract between medicine and society. In the medical humanities, this relationship has been studied primarily in historical terms (Porter; Rothman; Rothman, Marcus, and Kieluk), with some attention to cultural critique (Foucault; Goodman, McElligott, and Marks). The discourse of experimenting on the body, however, also takes place in the interaction of the contemporary medical encounter, where the discourse of medical research is presented to patients and families in the form of invitations to participate in medical research. Based on field research in cancer clinics and pediatric clinics, this paper will describe, analyze, and theorize the interactional discourses that make up a discourse of medical research in contemporary encounters.

Lisa Maruca (Interdisciplinary Studies) "Reading Bodies: New Technologies of Learning in Eighteenth-Century England"

This project seeks to chart the history of a disappearance: the learning body. At the same time, it will attempt to uncover the process of technological normalization that allowed print to rise as the medium of education. My focus is on the eighteenth century in England, when rising literacy rates and an increasingly commercial culture of print merged to produce an educated subject whose behaviors, achievements and accomplishments?indeed, one's very being?had been fed and sustained by a steady input of a specific, material consumer good, the print book. My hypothesis is that the rise of print learning came through the marginalization of alternate forms that more explicitly engaged physical activity and other somatic enactments. Further, the cultural, technological and commercial promotion of reading specifically cast that process as purely and intangibly mental, transcending both the material textual artifact and the embodied reader. Thus physical presence was evacuated from one crucial location, the book, where civilized subjectivity was thought to be expressed and mediated.

At the same time notions of the learning body were losing rhetorical power, however, alternate practices did persist. Certainly, in eighteenth-century England, books were not the only conveyor of information to be attained nor the only tool of socialization. To fully trace the demise in status of embodied forms of learning would require a study that traced the institutional rise of schooling?and its commitment to mass instruction of literacy?into the nineteenth and twentieth centuries. While my study does look ahead to this broader movement, however, I more specifically focus on an earlier moment in its history when this outcome was not yet inevitable, when the mind-body split was still being culturally negotiated.

One important venue for this struggle was actually print itself. During the eighteenth century, didactic fiction, sermons, how-to manuals, encyclopedias and other reference materials, personal rhetorics or secretaries, and collections of literature and grammars meant specifically for educational uses poured from the presses. These texts were the outgrowth of the profound technological and cultural change that is at once the organizing principal of Elizabeth Eisenstein's seminal The Printing Press as an Agent of Change, and one of its most neglected issues: what happened when, as she puts it, "learning by reading gained at the expense of learning by doing" (70). By the eighteenth century, a flourishing yet highly competitive print trade was prepared not only to meet customer needs, but to create new desires as they sought to specialize and diversify. The now capital-intensive trade, outgrowing its roots in the guild system, were led by a new breed of booksellers who not only sold books, but often oversaw the entire publication process, including the conceiving of projects for which they then commissioned writers. While didactic literature has a long tradition, this period witnessed the movement of print into heretofore separate spheres, as new books purported to re-make almost every aspect of culture, from manners to morals to education to work and trade, attempting to reach apprentice, master and gentleman alike. All they required to teach their useful lessons was literacy.

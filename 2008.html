Theme: Global Violence Impact and Resolution

Friday, November 21st
Alumni House

Please click here for schedule.

Explication

Violence itself is a very broad concept - ranging from individual to international conflict patterns. It involves the infliction of pain and suffering on life forms (human, animals) and the destruction of property and the natural environment. To narrow our focus we identify collective political violence with global or international implications (Global Violence) as the major concern of the world today. Our more specific interest here is on the impact or consequences of such violence on the human and global environment.

The main types of global violence to be investigated are international wars involving bombings (particularly cluster bombs), and at the domestic (national) level, civil wars and land mines, ethnic conflicts, and resource wars (over land, oil, diamonds etc.) in the Global South, particularly those conflicts in which the US and other developed global powers were/are directly involved both historically and contemporaneously.

A focus on the impact of collective violence counterbalances the plethora of existing theories on its causes and spread. The more immediate consequences involve in some cases genocide, in other cases refugee crises, deforestation and desertification, air, ground and water pollution, starvation, lost childhood, and untold numbers of casualties.

For the more economically developed societies such as the United States, the consequences of global violence could be viewed in terms of what Chalmers Johnson called the “blowback” effect which has brought economic crises, mass political protest, serious challenges to democratic participation and values, xenophobic responses to such issues as immigration and religious tolerance, global anti-Americanism and increasing terrorism.

In academia, the study of global violence transcends disciplinary boundaries. Apart from the Social Sciences (particularly Political Science and History) where probably most of the writing and studies of this phenomenon are pursued, the humanities and the arts also demonstrate concerns about wars and the consequences of wars and other forms of collective violence. Both war and peace have inspired creative expressions in music and song, films and plays, novels and poetry, and varieties of paintings. Also, in the natural sciences wars and collective violence significantly impact research on the environment and health issues.

Important among the consequences of global violence are the collective responses to it, particularly the varied approaches to conflict mitigation or resolution. Resolution of global violence could be conceived in terms of seeking alternatives to violence in struggles for change, such as non violent responses/approaches, instituting or expanding political democracy, and states employing more racially and socially inclusive economic redistribution programs and projects.

The Humanities Center is inviting WSU faculty to nominate themselves to present papers on the theme “Global Violence: Impact and Resolution” for the Center’s 2008 Fall Symposium on November 21, 2008. Each self-nomination must include the proposed title of the talk and short description (abstract) of the content of the paper including its theoretical framework, if applicable. This statement should not be long (a paragraph would suffice) but should contain enough information for the Center’s Advisory Board to make a decision on the papers to choose for the symposium. The proposals should be addressed to Walter Edwards, Director of the Humanities Center, 2226 FAB or email your proposal to walter.edwards@wayne.edu . The proposal should reach the Director by April 25, 2008. Authors of selected papers will be notified by May 9, 2008.

The Humanities Center welcomes proposals to talk about global violence from all disciplines.

Keynote Speakers

John Vasquez, Professor, Political Science at University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign
Controlling War: What Social Science Can Tell Us

The scientific study of peace and war has long been premised on the notion that to control war one must understand its causes; eliminating or mitigating those causes is the key to building peace. Recent research on the risk factors associated with crises escalating to inter-state war is reviewed to see what early warning indicators of war onset can be provided to help avoid or even prevent its outbreak. Research on zones of peace, especially the democratic peace but also work on stable borders, is reviewed to learn how to build peaceful neighborhoods. The dangers posed by deriving incorrect policy implications from the democratic peace are briefly outlined.

Rey Chow, Professor, Humanities at Brown University
“I Insist on the Christian Dimension”: Forgiveness, Translation, Secularized Representation, and the Outside of the Human

The question I will explore pertains to the type of exchange specific to human relations we call forgiveness. I shall begin with a reference to a key episode in the South Korean film “Miryang”/”Secret Sunshine” (directed by Lee Chang-dong, 2007), which stages forgiveness in a context of globalized Christian evangelism. Through the writings of Jacques Derrida, Erich Auerbach, and Edward Said, among others, I will then argue that the connotations of forgiveness extend considerably beyond a strictly religious dimension, going so far as to bear on questions about translation, the secularization of representation, and the limits of the human.

Speaker Abstracts

Irshad Altheimer, Assistant Professor, Criminal Justice
Social Disadvantage and Homicide in Global Context

Over the last 35 years immigration has led to the settlement of diverse ethnic groups in many Western developed nations. Although members of some of these groups have flourished, others have not fared as well. In many Western developed nations members of immigrant and ethnic minority groups face social disadvantage and live in isolated ethnic enclaves. Although this issue has received limited attention in the criminological literature, no study to date has adequately addressed whether change in levels of disadvantage for immigrants and ethnic minorities living in Western developed nations has influenced changes in homicide rates in these nations. This study attempts to address this gap in the literature.

Clifford Clark, Distinguished Visiting Professor, Economics
Development or Military Action as a Source of National Security

In a search for causes, I will review studies of organized violence during the first and second world wars. Recent works such as Dynamics of Destruction by Alan Kramer who argues that extensions of wars to include civil populations began during the early phases of the WWI, and Retribution: The Battle for Japan, 1944-45, by Max Hastings who seeks to explain violence directed to civilian populations by the combatants. I will review explanations of the authors, attempt to understand different forms of violence,?? and focus on common linkages between poverty, marginalization, humiliation, and violence described by the literature on the urban uprisings during the late 1960's, in development studies of poverty throughout the world including recent works on terrorism such as Civil Paths to Peace written byAmartya Sen, John Alderdice, Kwame Appian, and others. During the final weeks of study, I expect to put forth well defined intervening explanations for statistical ties between poverty and violence found by Paul Collier in The Bottom Billion.

Annie C. Higgins, Classical and Modern Languages, Literatures and Cultures
See the Faces, Hear the Voices

A popular Palestinian song describes martyr memorial posters thus:
The streets of the camp are crowded with portraits;
Our martyr speaks and gives voice to stones.

In the spirit of Ranajit Guha’s method of listening to the small voices, I will bring some of these neglected voices to challenge popular perceptions, and to replace some categorizations of “enemy” with that of “co-exister,” and even beyond that, to “friend.”
Public memorials for the dead have become a part of everyday life in refugee camps, most commonly as streams of posters which fade and are then replaced by faces of new martyrs. Is the martyr depicted as victim or victor? How many are fighters and how many are children and other civilians, including foreigners? Most significantly, what impact do the posters have on someone who sees them everyday? Is it the blowback phenomenon offered by media analyses, urging new generations to sacrifice themselves? Or do the posters become a part of the landscape that is largely ignored? I suggest that the responses are more nuanced than either of these explanations. We can find threads of engagement in the humane strains that become evident in the context of these poster texts.
I will discuss this context based on the time I spent in Jenin refugee camp in 2002, with reference to my research in refugee camps in the diaspora, especially Lebanon and Syria. I use de Certeau’s practice theory [The Practice of Everyday Life, 1984], Baudrillard’s concepts [Symbolic Exchange and Death, 1976], and Boal’s methods [Theatre of the Oppressed] in my theoretical framework.

When I did Boal-style theatre spontaneously in a variety of street and home venues, I was surprised at the depth of desire for making the enemy a friend. In practice, I have seen soldiers lay down their weapons to shake hands with children who put down their stones. The possibilities for the future are already happening in the present; the faces need to be seen and the voices heard. I hope to bring these into the spotlight for all to consider, and perhaps to reshape their own views on “what needs to be done.”

Frank Koscielski, Academic Services Officer IV
Teaching Non-Violence: an Interdisciplinary Approach

I have had the privilege of designing and implementing a large number of courses for the Department of Interdisciplinary Studies here at Wayne State over the past ten years.? A number of them, both at the undergraduate and graduate level, have dealt with war, national and international violence, and most importantly movements for peace and social justice. These courses include “World War I as a Turning Point” (3000 level), “Approaches to the Study of History” (3000 level), “Peaceful Revolutions: 20th Century Non-Violent Movements for Social Change” (3000 and 6000 level), and “The Cold War” (3000 and 7000 level).?

Each of these courses explores, in some way, humanity’s propensity for hatred, violence, and war. But each course also taught about the individuals and institutions who, using non-violent methods, sought to understand and oppose the forces of evil and violence in the world. In all of these courses I used multiple approaches and multiple disciplines to study and develop an understanding of, not only the causes, but possible solutions. Music, poetry, literature, film?the visual and performing arts as well as the Social Sciences, particularly History, Anthropology, and Economics were incorporated into the teaching and learning processes. Students were encouraged to grapple with the issues and seek their own understanding of violence and non-violence.

My presentation will focus on particular methodologies for teaching about violence and non-violence using my syllabi and course materials as examples of interdisciplinary approaches to problem solving. Education, global understanding, and cooperation provide the only long term hope for humanity’s survival on earth. In this presentation I will demonstrate how teaching non-violence can be an ideal way to both emphasize interdisciplinary methods of study and to nurture integration of knowledge towards positive change.

Perry Mars, Professor, Africana Studies ?
Hot Legacies of the Cold War: Destabilization and Change in the Caribbean

The food riots and protests in Haiti, Guyana, and other Caribbean countries in April 2008 are only the latest manifestations of the usually violent and destabilizing legacies of Cold War strategies employed in that region. This paper will seek to trace the inter-connection between the application of US cold war policies to the Caribbean between the 1950s and the 1980s, and the frequency of political violence events, labor unrest, armed criminal gangsterism, narco-trafficking, and hunger marches today - with the further consequence of increasing militarization of conflict in the most vulnerable of these Caribbean countries. The relationship between these “security” issues and the prospects for developmental change will also be addressed. US Cold War strategies are discerned not only in terms of fostering “low intensity warfare” in the region, but in the imposition of a “new world order” asserting IMF structural adjustment policies and the hegemonic control of neo-liberalist globalization. Further, this study puts under searching scrutiny, or also challenges, three basic theoretical assumptions of the main literature on global political violence: that violent conflicts between democracies are virtually non-existent, that coercive and military force is sufficient to bring peace and democracy to poor unstable countries, and that internal factors are invariably predominant in determining political conflict and violence in developing countries.??

Frederic S. Pearson, Director of Peace and Conflict Studies, and Susumu Suzuki, Ph.D. student, Political Science
Approaching Peace: Origins, Processes and Outcomes of Negotiations in Civil Wars, 1990-2006

This paper presents our research on disaggregating negotiation processes in civil wars in the context of external military intervention and arms supply in the conflicts. We seek to understand the effects of intervention and arms on the onset (timing), process (progress/regress) and outcome (success/failure) of civil war negotiations in Southeast Asia and other regions. Southeast Asia is the site of a high percentage of civil war cases in the post-cold war period. A careful theoretical framework for understanding negotiation impetus for both governments and insurgent groups is developed and presented for testing with quantitative and qualitative indicators. The framework will entail such factors as contrasting group and government perspectives, power balances, stalemates, costs, and external backing. Our findings show various dimensions affecting progress and failure in approaching peace, together with policy-relevant considerations for facilitating effective talks and conflict management. Results of a preliminary pilot study of five countries are appended; the entire group of Southeast Asian countries will be analyzed for the November presentation.??

Michael Scrivener, Professor, English
Habermas and Taylor on Global Violence

The usefulness and limitations of J?rgen Habermas's writings that are relevant to the problem of global violence are important to explore. Few thinkers have developed as rigorously as Habermas Kantian ideas of cosmopolitan right and norms of international law. In interviews and a few writings, he has applied these ideas and norms to contemporary international conflicts, including the first Gulf War, which he supported on grounds of international law, and the present war in Iraq, which he has opposed on the same legal grounds. For understanding the emotional intensities behind the fierce opposition to Israel one has to turn away from a Kantian like Habermas to a Hegelian like Charles Taylor, who attends to the process by which social groups acquire and maintain their identity. Abstractly and pragmatically, I am inquiring about the capacity of rational procedures and logic to mitigate against the violence of nationalism and religion. If one uses Taylor to understand nationalistic and religious commitments, do Habermasian cosmopolitan procedures require revision of any kind?? How does the rational cope with the non-rational when the latter is violent??

Anca Vlasopolos, ?Professor, English
Global Violence and the Creation of the Transnational Subject as Reflected in Contemporary Fiction and Memoirs

My presentation will center on the way in which a large number of novels and memoirs in the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries articulate the protagonist(s) as transnational subjects. The definition of a transnational subject, as I see it forming through these texts, is one whose antecedents are “mixed” or “impure” or of an ethnicity that constitutes a minority in whatever nation the subjects finds him/herself in. In such memoirs as Sara Suleri’s Meatless Days and Shirley Lim’s Among the White Moon Faces, as well as in novels like M.G. Vassanji’s The In-Between World of Vikram Lall and Arhundati Roy’s The God of Small Things, the protagonists (narrators in the case of the memoirs) struggle with problems of location in their search of identity. The crisis in adjustment, assimilation, successful migration, successful returns can in each case be seen as arising from global violence exacerbated in the country of origin by forces of colonialism and its aftermath; by global capital; by the rise of nationalism and religious fervor in response to global marginalization of small nation-states; by the continued practices of empire even after independence.

Because the scope of the presentation is brief, I will concentrate on showing the startling similarities of subject formation under global violence despite the minutely specific circumstances of the narrators (memoirs) and protagonists (novels). My research focuses on the phenomenon of the transnational subject in contemporary writing; its frequency suggests that notwithstanding national and religious fanaticism, the human condition in the new century tends toward a transnational identity that opposes global violence through two primary traits: a deliberate, conscientious alliance with people not of one’s own narrowly defined “family,” whether this means “race,” nationality, ethnic group; and a fierce loyalty to local culture in its best, most earth-embracing practices.

David Weinberg, Director of the Cohn-Haddow Center for Judaic Studies and Professor, History
Dealing with Holocaust Survivor Youth in Postwar West European Jewish Communities

Among the many issues that concerned Jewish communities in postwar Western Europe as they attempted to reintegrate returning survivors and new immigrants from Nazi camps was the fate of young survivors. Community leaders were convinced that the lack of adult supervision and the hostility that Jewish youth often evinced toward authority during the Holocaust had led many? of them to engage in anti-social activity along with their non-Jewish peers. The problem was seen as most acute among the thousands of young men and women orphaned by the war. In the late 1940s and early 1950s, Jewish educators in France, Belgium, and Holland talked openly about the danger of Jewish 'juvenile delinquency' and the fate of 'feral children.' Leaders of all ideological stripes wondered whether rebellious Jewish youths threatened the survival of the community by refusing to socialize with one another and to marry. As late as 1953, a report presented to the American Jewish Joint Distribution Committee warned that unless something was done to integrate 'alienated youth,' 'one can envision a period, close at hand, in which all Jewish efforts, in whatever sphere, will have lost all possibility to survive.'

In my presentation, I propose to discuss the distinctive problems that young Jewish survivors faced in readapting to the postwar environment in France, Belgium, and the Netherlands. Special emphasis will be placed on the various approaches devised by communal leaders to integrate young survivors back into the Jewish fold, including the development of informal methods of Jewish education, the establishment of children?s homes and summer camps, changes in religious liturgy and observance, and the creation of Jewish community centers.

Daphne Ntiri, Associate Professor of Africana Studies
Merry Stewart from University of Detroit College of Nursing,
Transformative Learning Intervention: Effect on Functional Health Literacy and Diabetes Knowledge in Older African Americans

This study evaluated the effect of a transformative learning (TL) intervention on functional health literacy and diabetes knowledge in older African Americans in Detroit. Twenty participants from senior community centers completed a six session intervention.

Research pertaining to adult learning theories has been centered in traditional educational environments (e.g. classrooms). However, adult learning theories are reaching beyond traditional contexts. These extended contexts include health care environments. The exploration of educational pedagogies for adults in health care has been driven by longer life spans and the increasing incidence of chronic illnesses (Chronic Illness - The Rise of Chronic Illness, 2007).

Educators have explored the process of change in adults over the years. Adult educators have been shifting from a complete reliance on traditional pedagogical approaches to alternative learning approaches (Knowles, 1984). Knowles stated that the goal of these alternative approaches should be to motivate adults to become more active in the learning process. Active learning affords adults the opportunities to develop new frames of reference and see the world differently from where they were before. Green (1981) advocated that active learning principles emphasize learner choice more than expert control.

Mezirow (1996) has expanded the theoretical debate further with his focus on transformative learning (TL). Mezirow declared that TL is a social process by which one can construct and apply new meaning to one's experience as a guide to action. In other words, TL is viewed as an effective method to expand one's consciousness and bring about the required social change as is advocated in this study. Additionally, TL is a self-directed process whereby learners use their initiatives with or without the help of others to determine the information they need and seek ways to acquire it (Cranton, 2006). This acquisition process involves self-reflection by the learners in which they question why it is important to learn and the validity of the learning itself (Pilling-Cormick, 1997). This process is also in support of Freire's (1970; 1973) theory of critical consciousness that promoted the application of transformative learning principles as an instrument for personal transformation and social change. Freire asserted that education is liberating for both the change agent and the individual learner in the search for knowledge.
